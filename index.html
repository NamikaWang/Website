  1. The weight of the bridge was 32.7 grams.
  2. The weight held by our bridge was 447 grams of water.
  3. The ratio of weight of bridge to weight held is 13.6.
  4. The base of spaghetti contributed the most weight to the bridge.
  5. The design was inspired by reasearch online from other experiments.
  6. The design worked well and held a good amount of weight but it held less than the trial bridge.
  7. The design held well but the sides were too thin which caused the bridge to fall.

    1/12/24

    On this week, we made Oobleck, which is a non-Newtonian fluid; it has properties of both liquids and solids. It is made out of cornstarch and water and it can behave like a solid or a liquid depending on how much pressure you apply. When you don't use much pressure and you movements are slow, the Oobleck behaves as a liquid, but if you use more pressure and have faster movements, the Oobleck acts as a solid.

    2/23/24

    This week we put together the second iteration of our project. We started with cutting out the cardboard base with the end having a 4-inch cut out to allow for space for the string to be able to wind up around the axle to create tension for the mouse trap to be able to pull the car forward. We cut the wooden skewers for the two axles into 9 inches each, we had to make sure the was enough space for the wheels to not be too close to the cardboard base or as we saw from the first interaction, the wheels would get caught and slow down drastically. The other wooden skewer was 5 inches to be glued onto the mousetrap. We cut the string into 10 inches because if there was too much, the extra string would cause problems such as getting caught in the wheels and axle. Then we glued all the pieces together. We started by gluing the 1-inch pieces of straw to the base to hold the axle, we used the axle to ensure the straws were aligned, if we had not aligned the straws together, the axle would not be straight which would slow the car down. After the straws were glued down we glued the CD wheels on. We glued the first wheel of each axle and had to make sure the wheel was aligned by putting the CD on top of a duct tape round, making sure the wooden skewer was aligned straight up. Then we tested the second iteration of our mouse trap car. Our estimated distance traveled is 24 feet and the result was 46 feet. We found that the way we wrapped the string around the axle dramatically affected the travel distance. If we wrapped the string tighter together, the distance was about double the distance if we wrapped the string loosely. We ran the project many times up until we hit a consistent average of about 35 feet.
